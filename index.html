Smart Fish Route Predictor: A Multi-Module Machine Learning Framework

AI-Driven Fisheries Decision Support Using Oceanography, Local Catch Intelligence, Market Trends, and Ecological Screening

Explore the Research Meet the Team

Research Objective: Smart Fish Route Predictor is a multi-module machine learning framework designed to support fisheries decision-making in small-scale fishing communities. By integrating oceanographic hotspot prediction, spawning-likelihood screening, market trend forecasting, and hyperlocal catch-success prediction, the system helps fishers decide where to fish, when to land catches, and when to apply ecological caution. The framework also includes a fish variety image detection module for new fishers, using YOLOv11 and a 30,000-image dataset to support image-based fish identification.

Project Domain & Methodology

Bridging machine learning and sustainable fisheries management.

Literature Survey

Traditional fisheries forecasting has relied heavily on habitat suitability models, linear regression, and ARIMA-style approaches. However, modern fisheries demand more robust methods capable of modeling the complex, non-linear interactions among oceanographic shifts, fish movement patterns, and volatile market behaviors that classical methods often fail to capture.

Research Gap

Most existing studies focus on isolated tasks: either hotspot mapping, biological spawning analysis, market prediction, or local catch modeling. Very few initiatives have successfully integrated these multi-dimensional factors into a single, coordinated framework capable of providing holistic decision support for small-scale fisheries.

Research Problem

Small-scale fishers require practical, real-time decision support to navigate rapidly changing ocean conditions and volatile economic environments. Currently, available tools are fragmented, leaving fishers to rely on intuition rather than a combined stream of spatial, ecological, and economic intelligence.

Research Objectives

  • ✓ To identify high-potential fishing hotspots using oceanographic and catch data.
  • ✓ To forecast short-term fish market trends for landing-time optimization.
  • ✓ To screen potentially sensitive spawning-favorable areas using proxy ecological indicators.
  • ✓ To predict hyperlocal catch success using Point Pedro CPUE and local environmental conditions.
  • ✓ To identify fish varieties using image-based computer vision for new fishers.
  • ✓ To integrate all modules into a unified fisheries decision-support framework.

Methodology

The system utilizes a structured data pipeline: data is harvested from diverse sources (satellite oceanography, market records, and manual fishing logs), followed by intensive preprocessing and cyclic feature engineering. XGBoost serves as our central machine learning backbone for spatial and economic modules, while YOLOv11 is used for fish variety image detection. A fish image dataset of 30,000 images is used to support practical fish identification through computer vision. The final output is an integrated advisory workflow that translates model probabilities and visual detections into actionable fishing routes.

Dataset and Data Sources

This project integrates multiple datasets to support fisheries decision-making across hotspot prediction, ecological screening, market forecasting, and hyperlocal catch-success prediction. Each module uses a different but complementary data source, allowing the framework to combine oceanographic intelligence, catch-based signals, market behavior, and localized fishing-ground conditions within one coordinated decision-support workflow.

Module 01

Oceanographic and Catch Dataset for Hotspot Prediction

The hotspot prediction module uses an integrated catch–environment dataset developed from Indian Ocean historical catch records combined with oceanographic variables. The dataset contains 82,962 observations collected at the cell–month level, where each record links catch-weight outcomes with environmental conditions and sampling metadata. The main predictors include sea surface temperature (SST), sea surface salinity (SSS), sea surface density (SSD), sea surface height (SSH), chlorophyll-a, month-based seasonal encodings, species code, and monsoon category. Bathymetry and depth-related information are also included as part of the modeling workflow.

Data Sources: Indian Ocean catch records, Copernicus CDS (cds.climate.copernicus.eu), GEBCO 2025 sub-ice dataset
Purpose: Identify high-potential fishing cells across the Indian Ocean to support "where to fish" decisions.
  • Total records: 82,962
  • Train/Val/Test: 49,776 / 16,593 / 16,593
  • Grid: 0.5-degree latitude–longitude
Module 02

Proxy Catch–Environment Dataset for Spawning-Likelihood Screening

The proxy spawning-likelihood module uses a supervised dataset derived from catch–environment observations. Since direct biological spawning labels were not available, the model uses a deterministic high-catch proxy label to estimate areas that may require ecological caution. The feature space includes latitude, longitude, depth, sea surface salinity (SSS), sea surface density (SSD), sea surface temperature (SST), sea surface height (SSH), chlorophyll-a, month encodings, monsoon encoding, and several interaction terms. This module should be interpreted as a conservation-oriented proxy screening component rather than direct biological confirmation of spawning activity.

Data Sources: Derived from catch–environment records + Copernicus CDS + GEBCO 2025
Purpose: Flag potentially sensitive spawning-favorable areas for conservation-aware harvesting.
  • Total samples: 82,962
  • Model type: XGBoost classifier
  • Role: Ecological screening / caution flagging
Module 03

Peliyagoda Fish Market Price Dataset

The market forecasting module uses weekly species-level fish price records collected from the Peliyagoda Fish Market in Sri Lanka from 2019 to 2025. The raw dataset was compiled from 256 official weekly Excel sheets. Each record includes fish name information, wholesale price in Rs/kg, previous week price references, previous year price references, and percentage changes. After preprocessing, cleaning, and standardization, the final dataset contained 7,762 observations across 54 commercially important fish species.

Data Sources: Official weekly market records from Peliyagoda Fish Market, Sri Lanka (2019–2025)
Purpose: Forecast short-term fish price trends for landing-time optimization.
  • Total observations: 7,762
  • Coverage: 54 fish species
  • Model: XGBoost / Ensemble Voting Regression
Module 04

Point Pedro CPUE and Weather Dataset

The hyperlocal catch-success module uses a ground-aware dataset created from Point Pedro fishing logs together with weather-related environmental variables. The local fishing logs were digitized into a structured dataset containing date, fishing ground, catch quantity, and effort-related information. The module also incorporates daily weather covariates such as wind speed, wind direction, pressure, and precipitation. This dataset supports localized prediction at the community fishing-ground level and captures conditions that directly influence nearshore fishing success.

Data Sources: Point Pedro fishing logs / digitized fishery records, OpenWeather API
Purpose: Predict local catch success for productive fishing trips at the community scale.
  • Total records: 948
  • Weather: wind, pressure, precipitation
  • Role: Hyperlocal fishing-ground recommendation
Module 05

Fish Variety Image Dataset

The fish variety image detection module uses a large-scale image dataset designed for computer vision-based fish identification. The dataset contains 30,000 fish images and is used to train a YOLOv11-based model for fish variety recognition. This module is intended to support new fishers by improving species identification through image-based detection.

Data Sources: Fish image dataset prepared for fish variety image detection and recognition.
Purpose: Identify fish varieties from images for new fishers.
  • Total images: 30,000
  • Model: YOLOv11
  • Accuracy: 76%
